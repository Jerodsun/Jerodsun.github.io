Ars Scriptura: Three Guidelines for Powerful Writing

What do you think when you read this?

“It is perhaps worth nothing that adherence to a purposive image of the process by which revolutions develop coaxes even theories intended to be social-structural into social-psychological explanations. (pg 16, States and Social Revolutions, Skocpol)



Unfortunately, you’ll read thousands of these sentences in college.

Don’t write like this. Your teacher will not appreciate it.

By keeping three simple guidelines in mind, you’ll have a better writing experience. And a better grade.



Three easy concepts:

1. Cohesion

aka: your writing must flow.



  • Start with familiar concepts.
  • Connect these to new concepts.
  • “Old to new” applies to sentences and paragraphs.
  • Keep the ball rolling.
  • Remove distractions.

Imagine your sentences fitting together like individual pieces in a jigsaw puzzle. Isn’t it satisfying when they do?

Cohesion in action coming up: we’ll apply these guidelines to a short passage.

OCaml is a multi-paradigm language primarily supporting functional programming with parametric polymorphism. It is type-safe and does not require explicit declaration. Compilation generates bytecode passed to ocamlcc. Jane Street has created their own standard library for OCaml.


2. Audience

But but but it makes sense! (To me)

Five days later:


Understand your audience.

  • No need to prove that we can use a thesaurus.
  • Our audience isn’t a room of academics. (Even if they were, academics like clarity too.)

Practice time!

I still don’t understand that quote on top…

Let’s rewrite in a way that’s comprehensible.

Cohesion:

  • “Theories” and “revolution” are common terms. We want to talk about them.
  • Don’t use “purpositive,” “adherence,” or “coaxes”. (how does adherence to a purpositive image even coax?)
  • Remove distractions: “It is perhaps worth noting…”

Ok, what did we come up with?

“Traditional theories on revolution rely on social and structural explanations, but nevertheless account for the psychology of human nature.”

At least that makes sense.

Example: Cohesion and Audience in Action

Below is something a programmer might say:

OCaml is a multi-paradigm language primarily supporting functional programming with parametric polymorphism. It is type-safe and does not require explicit declaration. Compilation generates bytecode passed to ocamlcc. Jane Street has created their own standard library for OCaml.

  • Hol up.
  • Wait, what does this even mean?



Our audience does not want technospeak. Let’s also add some cohesive glue according to the guidelines above.

You’ve probably heard of several programming languages. But have you heard of OCaml? OCaml is a programming language that encompasses a niche in software development. After you write human-readable code, OCaml translates it to machine-readable code through a program known as a “compiler”. OCaml has two unique advantages. If you say something must be an integer, then it can never act as a decimal. But you don’t have to explicitly say it – Ocaml will infer it for you. Not all programming languages have this “type-safe” quality. These qualities have made it the language of choice for Jane Street, a quantitative trading firm. Jane Street is now the main sponsor of OCaml development.

Blah blah blah. Trust me, this is better.


Now you know what I’m trying to say. And maybe you want to learn OCaml?

Ok, you don’t. But you get the point.

Just one more concept!

3. Concision

Before:

In my personal opinion, it is imperative that we choose our words sparingly in order to present a clear and compelling argument so that our readers, who we might believe begin reading with great motivation, will have an easier time to grasp and understand the main concepts that we present.

After:

See below.

Conclusion:

  • Your writing must flow.
  • Consider your audience.
  • Be Concise.
